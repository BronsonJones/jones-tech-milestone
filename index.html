Top 5 Tech Milestones

The Complex Number Calculator is completed (1940)

The first ever computing relay was completed in 1937. This led to the discovery that computers can easily learn to compute different numbers easily and without problem. When this was discovered, many computer scientists wanted to expand these relays into larger, more complex computers. These bigger computers could very easily solve huge, complex multiplication and division problems. Despite the benefits, many would start to reject the idea of trying to build and manufacture them due to the fact that the relays that would be used were incredibly expensive at the time. When Bell Telephone Labs heard of this new advancement in computing, they were intrigued. They needed a new way to solve their growing and complex mathematical problems. George Stibitz, a researcher at Bell Labs, eventually finished the designs and they began production. In 1940, Stibitz demonstrated the CNC at an American Mathematical Society conference. All calculations could be done remotely.


Introduction of the IBM 650 Calculator (1954)

Being possibly the first mass produced computer, IBM sold over 450 of them in just one year. This model was incredibly popular with universities and their students. The IBM 650 was where the first ever generation of students were taught hot to program and code computers and other machinery. The most probable reason that this specific model of calculators was widely accepted and loved by the public was that it was easily the fastest calculator at the time. It used a magnetic data-storage drum that spun at a rate of over 12,500 rotations per minute.


LaserDisc is Released (1978)

The LaserDisc was one of the first forms of optic disc storage access that was first sold in the 1970’s. It was labeled and marketed as “MCA Discovision” and was a commercial home video format. Weighing in at a foot in diameter, the discs could hold huge amounts of information. Inline with the size, the players needed to view these discs were gigantic and expensive. On the other hand, the CD-ROM, which would be released a few years later, required a much smaller, less expensive player, however, it only held a fraction of the storage that these larger discs did. Due to this, the LaserDiscs were only really only put in government and educational use.


The Start of MySpace (2003)

Despite not being the largest, or first, social networking site, MySpace was the most trafficked website for many years. Friendster, an earlier social networking site, was the basis for MySpace. The developers, Chris DeWolfe and Tom Anderson, wanted to add more features and fix things that they thought would make the social network better. They added features such as file storage, small video games, music playback, and much, much, more. In 2005, the number of Myspace users grew to and above 100 million users. However, it soon came to an end with the beginning of Facebook. MySpace paved the way for the many, many different types of social media and networking sites that exist today.


Angry Birds becomes the top-selling game (2010)

While the use and marketing of touch-screen smartphones and tablets rose, many people were looking for pastimes that could be easily accessed with them. Angry Birds was were almost everyone found a happy place by either just slinging these birds across the screen or actually trying to complete the whole story and all the levels with it. Angry Birds was THE game that started all mobile gaming.
